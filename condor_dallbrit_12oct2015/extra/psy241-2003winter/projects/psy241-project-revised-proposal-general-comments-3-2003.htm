General Comments on Revised Project Proposals

 

  1. Inter-rater reliability
    1. Have 2 people (coders) use your content analysis protocol to assign values to your DV for all the ads, and record the data in your data summary sheet.  (in columns labeled “coder1 DV”,  “coder2 DV” for example). 
    2. Calculate the percentage of the time that the 2 coders agreed, and report this in your results section.  This is your measure of inter-rater reliability.
    3. For any cases the two of you disagreed on, resolve the disagreement or exclude those ads.
    4. Use the resulting data in your analyses to test your hypothesis

 

  1. Outliers – It makes no sense to look for outliers in a categorical DV

 

  1. The “References” section comes before the Appendices and Tables.

 

  1. Each appendix starts on a new page and has a descriptive title:  “Appendix A:  Content Analysis Protocol.”

 

  1. References must be cited in the text of the paper (usually in the Intro).

 

  1. Table
    1. If your DV is categorical, you have 2 choices:

                                                               i.      In a 2x2 table, report how many ads there were in each cell defined by crossing the IV and DV values: male ads coded “yes”, male ads coded “no”, female ads coded “yes” and female ads coded “no”, for example if your IV was gender and your DV was coded categorically into “yes” and “no” categories.

                                                              ii.      In a 1x2 table, report the % of ads coded “yes” on your DV for men and for women (assuming your IV is gender).

    1. If your DV is quantitative, you should have a table of means, with one mean for each level of your IV (the mean of male ads and the mean of female ads, for example, if your IV is gender).

 

  1. Plagiarism vs. citing sources:  2 reasons for a citation: to support a claim of fact and to give credit for others’ ideas. 
    1. What is plagiarism?

                                                               i.      Presenting an idea without giving credit for where you found it. 

                                                              ii.      Presenting factual information without citing the source of the information.  A claim of fact always requires a citation unless it is a) common knowledge, or b) it is a finding from data you collected and are presenting in this paper for the first time.

                                                            iii.      Using someone else’s exact words (more than 2 or 3 in a row) without using quote marks and citing the source and page.

    1. When must you include a citation in your writing?

                                                               i.      When you are presenting an idea from anywhere but your own imagination – cite the article where the idea came from.

                                                              ii.      When you are making a claim of fact, for anything that is not common knowledge.
