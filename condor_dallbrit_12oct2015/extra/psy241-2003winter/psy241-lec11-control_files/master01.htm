Confounds: the only possible confounds in the assignment process are those that might arise due to chance.  Such confounds are unlikely in a large enough sample, and are very unlikely to be repeated in a replication of the experiment.
Only when correlated substantially:  otherwise, you will lose too much statistical power (because you will have to use a matched-pairs test, which uses half as many data points since it considers each pair as an observation rather than each individual as an observation).  [note: can demonstrate this with “example-data-control” demo data files; original has matched pairs (members of pairs are correlated on DV), “unmatched pairs” files have the same data in pairs that are not correlated, set up for either dependent or independent t-tests.]
Howto: variable correlated with DV:  dv = number of bar presses in rat study
Correlated variable:  activity level (more active rats press the bar more in general)
Counterbalancing:  things that can not be the same for all subjects in the experiment are counterbalanced so that they occur an equal number of times in all conditions (levels of the IV).
This makes sure they are not confounded with the IV
Example:  2 experimenters in a between-subjects experiment on reading comprehension.  Have half the control subjects with experimenter A and half with experimenter B, and same for experimental condition.  Then include “experimenter” as another IV in the design of the experiment for analysis.
Example (from text)
Can’t randomly assign students to classrooms; can’t match and randomly assign to classrooms.  But you know IQ will be correlated with grades.  So use a statistical technique to equate all students grades based on IQ – in other words to “remove” the effect of IQ from the data and see what effects on grades are left to account for with the two teaching techniques (the 2 levels of the IV).
