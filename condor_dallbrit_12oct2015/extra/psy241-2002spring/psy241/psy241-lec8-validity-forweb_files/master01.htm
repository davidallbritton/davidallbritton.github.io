History:
1 study of perceptions of threat of terrorism; men interviewed first, then women, plane hijacking occurs in between. 2 test one condition of learning expt this week, the other next week.  Possible problems?
Maturation – particularly problematic in longitudinal research
Testing effects
- practice effects
-problem with repeated measures or repeated testings
-
Selection Bias – can introduce a confound if exp and control groups are different somehow
Mortality – those who drop out will be different from those who don’t. 
Regression to the mean – when high scores become lower or low scores become higher when retested, due solely to statistical reasons.
** Do demo for reg to the mean
Interpret differently:  hawthorne effect; doing a different expt. Than you intended (eg: doing an expt on memory for text and they think it is actually a social psych expt on something else, could change the way they read the texts) Good subject: trying to respond the way they think the experimenter wants them to (even if they have guessed the purpose of the expt for example)
Evaluation apprehension:  changing their responses to try to look “normal” or competent (example: when students think everything is either an IQ test or a test for mental illness).
Non-representative samples:  mostly college students.  If we draw conclusions about how people learn from texts, for example, are college students representative?
Changes over time: changes in social norms (dirty word study); changes in educational level over time, amount of experience people have with written language could affect studies of how people read – with more education and experience with reading, some processes could become more automated…
Other ways: these are covered in stats courses.  Violations of assumptions of statistical tests are a big one; capitalizing on change (through post-hoc tests or “file drawer” effects) is another.
