Reliability: consistency in the measurements of a test
Validity: ability of a test to measure what it is supposed to measure
Example: IQ
Observed score is your IQ (say its 115)
True score is your *real* IQ (say it’s 120)
Systematic Error could be time of day you were tested (eg: if people tested early do 5 pts worse than tested later) Random Error: even if you took it the same time of day, every time you took it the score would be different – measurement error, mistakes, noises in environment, scoring errors, variations in your arousal and attention level, etc.
Systematic error can include confounds if they vary with the IV, or can be less damaging systematic variations that affect all levels of the IV equally Increasing the variability associated with the construct of interest increases reliability
Decreasing error variance increases reliability.

*** But even if we maximize reliability, reliability doesn’t tell us *anything* about *what* we are measuring!
For that we need to talk about validity.
Face – appears to measure what it claims
Example: mmpi has little face validity
Not really related to true validity
Content – should sample the full range of behavior represented by the theoretical construct being measured
Criterion – should relate closely to other measures of the same construct
Concurrent – the measure’s ability to distinguish between groups it should be able to distinguish. Example: intelligence test should distinguish between good and poor students (correlate with current grades). Predictive: measure the variable’s ability to predict something it theoretically should predict (correlate with a future measure related to the same construct). Example: intelligence test should predict who will do well in college.
Convergent – does it converge with other measures of the same construct?  Does your IQ test rank a sample of people in the same order as the WAIS? Discriminant – it should not correlate with unrelated concepts (your IQ test should not correlate with extroversion).
