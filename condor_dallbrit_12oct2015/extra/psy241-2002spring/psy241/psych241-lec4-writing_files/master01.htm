Clarity – most important; in order to maximize objectivity, research results must be reported clearly and unambiguously so that others can evaluate and replicate them.
Authors – does the order of authors mean anything?  Who gets to be an author on a paper?
Abstract – summarizes the problem, method, results, and conclusion.  Is reprinted in the psych abstracts (psycinfo).
Intro – state the general problem and how it fits into previous research.  Say why the problem is important and interesting.  State your hypothesis and predictions (expected results).  Method – describes what was done in the study, in enough detail for someone else to evaluate the validity of the study and to replicate it.
Subsections described in next slide.
Results: describe how you analyzed the data, and present the findings.  What to include is on another slide.
Discussion – interpret the results’ meaning and significance. 
References: list all and only those sources cited in the paper
Footnotes:  Author note is always included (not numbered) to provide contact information and acknowledge grant support.
Tables & Figures – present summaries of the data and/or examples of materials.
Other subheadings may be appropriate for some studies (eg: “measures” in an expt that used several measures of IQ)
Participants: how many and how you got them.  Any characteristics relevant to the research (eg: native english speakers for language studies).
Apparatus – rarely given its own subheading unless it is particularly complicated or special; often included with materials.
Materials – describe the stimuli used; often give examples in a table for language studies.
Design – the experimental design.  IV and DVs, states the logic of the experiment.  Which variables were randomized, which counterbalanced.
Procedure – step by step account of what happened in the session and how the experiment was carried out. 
Displaying the data -- ** almost never includes the raw data.  Usually some sort of summary statistics (means, correlations, etc)
Summary statistics:  mean, correlation coeff.
May be in text or as a reference to a table.
Test of sign:  every claim must be justified statistically
Measure of variability: so that other researchers can reconstruct more of the data to reanalyze; also for use in a meta-analysis.
May be in a table (as SD, MSE, or SEM) or as error bars on a figure.
Authors – initials only
Although some parts of apa style are arbitrary or discipline-specific, the general plan of an article is a good model for any kind of communication, including a speech, a sales pitch, a presentation to a client…
