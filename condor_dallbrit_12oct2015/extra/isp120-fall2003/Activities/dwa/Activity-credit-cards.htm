Part V.   Investments

The Typical American invests a significant amount of his or her savings in stocks.  The 1990’s were an exceptionally good time for stock investors, with the Dow-Jones Industrial Average increasing about 290% from 1990 to 2000 (although it then fell over 30% in the next couple of years).  Historically, the stock market has averaged a return of about 7% per year.  Let’s assume that the next 10 years will be above average, and stocks will increase 10% per year.  Further assume that Mr. or Ms. Typical American (T. A.) has just gotten an unexpected $4000 tax rebate check. 

a.  How much money will T.A. have in 10 years if she invests it in the stock market, assuming a 10% increase per year?

b.  T.A. could instead choose to pay off the $4000 she owes on her credit card.  If T.A. puts the $4000 in the stock market, she will not be able to pay anything towards her credit card bill except for what she actually purchases each month.  If T.A. pays exactly the amount she spends each month, at the end of 10 years how much will T.A. owe on her credit card (assuming an 18% APR compounded monthly, and an initial balance of $4000)?

c.  How would you advise T.A. to invest her $4000?

d.  (Optional:  Extra Credit).  Some students have objected that this example is not realistic because the credit card company will not allow you to let the balance continue to increase – at a minimum you must pay the interest as well as paying for your purchases each month.  A more realistic comparison might be between these two options:

I)              Invest the $4000 in the stock market now, and continue to carry a balance of about $4000 on your credit cards each month.  Each month you manage to scrape up enough money to pay for your purchases, plus the interest on the $4000 credit card balance.  You have no income left over after paying the credit card bills, so at the end of the 10 years all the money you have is what has accumulated in your stock fund.  At the end of 10 years, you take $4000 out of your stock fund and pay off your credit card balances.

II)            Pay off the full $4000 of credit card balances now, rather than investing it in the stock market.  You then have enough income each month to pay for your purchases in full, plus you have left over the $60 that you no longer have to pay in interest each month.  You take this $60 each month and invest it in the stock account.

Assume that interest on the stock account compounds annually, and that you only receive interest on money that has been in the account for the full year.  Also assume that the stock account returns a gain of 10% every year, and that the credit card interest rate is 1.5% per month.  How much money would you have at the end of 10 years using each option?  Is there any point in the 10 years when your net worth (your assets minus your debts) would be greater using option I than option II?  (Include your Excel table that you used to calculate these answers in your word document).  Which is the better investment, paying off your credit card balances or buying stocks?
