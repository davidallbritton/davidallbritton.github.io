Activity 11: Initial Data Analysis for Project

Open a word document, write the names of your project members at the top.  

1.  Go to the U.S. Bureau of Census site and browse through the different sections of the 2000 U.S. Statistical Abstract. (The sections are listed in the left column; to view them, click on the numbers in the column next to the corresponding section).  Choose a general topic/section  that you will analyze for your project.   List this topic/section in your word document (you may just list the name of the section; for example, "Health" or "Education").  Also write your topic on the board so that we can see all the topics that are being chosen (we'd like to avoid duplicate topics).

2.  In your chosen section, look at some of the data tables and describe them as follows: 

a. What are the types of data that are contained in the section?  List two or three examples with a general description (for example, "In Vital Statistics, the data describes birth rates, death rates, marriages, etc..."). 

b. Which data sets do you find particularly interesting?  Why are these interesting to you; what about them makes you want to investigate them further?

3.  Choose two or three data tables that you would like to begin analyzing for your project.   List the titles or topics of the tables and tell why you have chosen these--what info is helpful in them? How do they pertain to the topic?   (NOTE:  you don't have to stick with these tables for the rest of your project; this is just to get you started)

4.  Choose one of these data tables to analyze in depth.  You can analyze these tables by opening them in MS Excel.  To do this, you'll need to find the table number in the corresponding section in the directory of files that the Quantitative Reasoning Center has set up for this project at http://qrc.depaul.edu/StatisticalAbstract2000List.asp (Note that the tables are labeled according to the year, then the table number. The last four digits of the file name refer to the table number in the Statistical Abstract; for example the file 00S0580.xls is the Excel version of table 580 in the 2000 abstract.   Save one of tables onto your disk (if you do not have a disk, save it to the desktop for now).  

a.  In your word document, describe the characteristics and format of the table.   For example, you might want to note the following:

  • The number and name of the table and the topic that the data describes; any special notes about the data
  • The years listed in the table: earliest, most recent, and the increments
  • The main categories of the data (by state? by demographic category? by topic? etc.)    
  • The form of the data (raw numbers?  percentages?  rates?)  

b.  Make a graph summarizing all or even just one part of the data in this table (such as the last 5 years, or a couple of the categories).  Remember to include axis labels and a title; please include the chart number in your title.  Paste your graph into your word document, and describe the graph:  what does it summarize?  what trends are evident? what are its interesting features?

5.  In your word document, write a paragraph about what your learned about this topic from your initial data analysis.  What questions does this raise about the topic?  What else would you like to explore?  (Note: Just brainstorm! This question will help you as you plan your paper)
