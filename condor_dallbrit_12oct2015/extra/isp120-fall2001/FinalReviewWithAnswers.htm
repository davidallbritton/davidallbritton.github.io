2.  In the period January 2000 to March 2000, stock for Sapient Corporation, a consulting company that helps businesses put their operations on the internet, dropped 51% and then rose by 49%.  By how many percent did price change over the whole period?

Answer. Starting at 100%, the stock dropped to 49% of its original value.  It then increase by 49% of the 49%, i.e., .49*.49=.2401 or 24.01% of the original price. Thus in the end it was 49% + 24.01% or 73.01% of its original value.   It was down 26.99%.

4. Carefully describe what the CPI is, how it is constructed, and what the meaning of actual index numbers is (e.g., the CPI in 1999 was 166.6; what does 166.6 mean?)

Answer. I highly recommend you read the CPI Tutorial.  However, I'll say this.  The CPI is number used to measure the changing value of money.   Economists select an imaginary "market basket" of goods and services that represents the buying patterns of most people.  Every month they collect price data on the items in the basket and essentially compute the value of the entire basket. The CPI is the ratio of the price of the basket in a given year to the price of the basket in a chosen base year multiplied by 100.  For example, the 166.6 CPI in 1999 means that the price of the standard market basket is 1.666 times as much as it was in the base period (1982-84).  It means that in 1999 the same goods and services cost about 66.6% more than they did in 1982-84.

7. Open the CigarettesBirthWeight.xls file and answer the following questions using your knowledge regarding associations between two variables (correlation).

a.       Use an x-y scatterplot to determine whether there is a correlation between cigarette smoking (by the mother) and low birth weight. Describe the relationship between these two variables based upon the correlation obtained.   Answer. There is negative correlation: the more cigarettes smoked, the lower the birthweight.

b.      Calculate the r-squared value of this correlation.  What does that tell you about smoking and birth weight? Answer. The r-squared value is about .78, which indicates a fairly strong negative correlation.

c.       Does the correlation necessarily mean that smoking causes low birthweight?  What are some possible common underlying causes? Answer. No, it does not mean that smoking causes low birthweight. Correlation does not imply causality! There could be common underlying causes, for example, mothers who smoke might tend to be younger, and younger women have a slightly higher tendency to have low birthweight babies.  Mothers who smoke might tend to come from less privileged socioeconomic groups, and these groups tend to have lower birthweight babies.  To have more confidence that smoking causes low birthweight, we would have to rule out alternative causes such as these.
