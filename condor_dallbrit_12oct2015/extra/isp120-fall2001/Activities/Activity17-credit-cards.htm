Part IV.   The Typical American Consumer

The typical American credit card user (T.A.) has about $4000 in revolving credit card debt.  That means that he or she carries a balance of $4000 from month to month on credit cards.  In the table above, replace the starting balance of $300 with the typical American’s balance of $4000. 

a.  After eight months, what is the balance on the credit card?

b.  What are the total payments, total expenses, and total interest charges for the whole 8 months?

c.  Does T.A. owe more or less than $4000 at the end of the 8 months?  By how much did T.A.’s payments exceed her expenses during those 8 months?  What conclusions can you draw from this example?

Part V.   Investments

The typical American has invested more and more in the stock market during the past decade.  The 1990’s were an exceptionally good time for stock investors, with the Dow-Jones Industrial Average increasing about 290% from 1990 to 2000.  Although it is very unlikely that stocks will do that well during the next ten years, let’s assume that they will – assume that over the next 10 years the stock market will return a 290% profit on whatever is invested today.  Further assume that Mr. or Ms. Typical American has just gotten an unexpected $4000 tax rebate check. 

a.  How much money will T.A. have in 10 years if she invests it in the stock market, assuming a 290% increase?

b.  T.A. could instead choose to pay off the $4000 she owes on her credit card.  If T.A. puts the $4000 in the stock market, she will not be able to pay anything towards her credit card bill except for what she actually purchases each month.  If T.A. pays only exactly the amount she spends each month, at the end of 10 years how much will T.A. owe on her credit card (assuming an 18% APR compounded monthly, and an initial balance of $4000)?

c.  How would you advise T.A. to invest her $4000?
