Activity 22: Categorical Data Analysis: Crosstabs

Week 9

In this activity you will expand your ability to analyze categorical data by producing cross-tabulations (crosstabs, for short). Crosstabs are by far the most common way to make comparisons across classifications.  In surveys, the classifications are usually age, race, gender, party affiliation, etc.  Once again we use the Metro Chicago Information Center Metropolitan Chicago Survey data.

1. Open the file MCIC-SMOKE.xls.   This file consists of the 1993 and 1997 responses for the question "Do you smoke now?" (SMOKE) along with a set of demographic variables: age (AG), race/ethnicity (RACETH), income level (INC4GP), religious affiliation (REL1), educational level (RESPED), and  gender (SEX01),  Note that the 1993 and 1997 data are on separate pages; there is also a code page that tells you what the codes mean. Familiarize yourself with the codes.

a. The first question we will ask is: What percentage of the sample of Chicago metropolitan area residents smoked in 1993 and in 1997? Using what you learned in Activity 15, find out. (You'll need to construct two pivot tables.)

b. Now we will extend our technique.  We would like to find out what percentage of men smoked and what percentage of women smoked in the sample in 1997.  A question like this is called a cross-tabulation, because the result is usually represented in a diagram that looks like:
