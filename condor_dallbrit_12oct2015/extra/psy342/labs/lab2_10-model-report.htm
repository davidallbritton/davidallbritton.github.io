Method

Participants.  Nine DePaul undergraduates (7 female and 2 male) participated in the experiment as part of a course requirement. 

Apparatus.  The experiment was conducted on PCs in a DePaul public  computer lab.  Presentation of materials and recording of responses was controlled by the MEL Lab software for experiments in psychology. [this section is optional]

Materials and Design.  The experiment was a single-factor within-subjects design.  The two levels of the independent variable (test type) were recognition and recall.  The dependent variables were percent correct and percent intrusions.  Percent correct was the primary measure of interest.  A list of 24 pairs of weakly semantically associated words such as “FRUIT flower” (from Tulving & Thompson, 1973) comprised the study list.  The first word of each pair (the cue) was presented in all caps, and the second (target) word was in lower case.  The recognition test consisted of the 24 target words plus three closely semantically associated distractors for each target word.  The 96 words in the recognition test were presented in 6 columns of 16 words each, and the words appeared to be arranged in random order within the recognition list.  For the cued recall test, the 24 primes from the study list served as cues.

         Procedure.  In the initial study session the 24 cue-target pairs were displayed in two columns of 12 pairs each.  Each cue was presented in all caps to the left of its associated target word, which was presented in lower case.  Participants were instructed to study the list for one minute, after which there would be a memory test.  Participants were also told that only accuracy (and not speed) was important in the experiment. 

After participants studied the list for one minute, a list of 96 words was presented and participants were instructed to indicate which of the words they remembered seeing in the study list.  Participants used the tab key to move the curser from one word to the next and used the plus key on the numeric keypad to select the words they recognized.  After making their selections, participants pressed the q key to end the recognition test. 

Immediately following the recognition test, the 24 cue words from the study session were presented in all caps, and participants were instructed to type the first and last letter of each target word that they could remember in the blanks next to the cue words.  They were also told that their responses could be typed in any blank – target words did not have to be matched to the correct cue words in order to be counted as correct. 

Results and Discussion

Percent correct responses and percent intrusions for the recognition and recall tests were calculated for each participant.  Percent correct was defined as the percent of the target words recognized or recalled.  Percent intrusions was defined as the percent of the participant’s responses that were incorrect on each test.  Mean percent correct responses and intrusions are reported in Table 1 and Figure 1 (from the MEL workbook).  The percentages of correct responses for each participant were analyzed in a paired t-test.  Test type (recognition vs. recall) was the within-subjects factor in the analysis. 

Based on previous research, I predicted a higher percentage of correct responses in the recall test than in the recognition test.  This prediction was not confirmed.  The trend was in the opposite of the predicted direction, with 57.4% correct (SD = 20.1) in the recognition test and 45.3% correct (SD = 21.4) in the recall test.  This difference was not significant, t (8) = 1.57, p > .10.  Thus the percent correct data revealed no evidence of recognition failure (better performance on cued recall than recognition), and the trend suggested the opposite: better recognition than recall performance.

Analysis of the percent intrusion data also indicated better performance in recognition than recall.  A paired t-test in which test type was the within-subjects factor revealed significantly fewer intrusions in the recognition test (24.4%, SD = 9.86) than in the recall test (43.11, SD = 25.11), t (8) = -2.64, p < .05.  Taken together, the percent correct and percent intrusion data support the conclusion that memory performance was better in the recognition test than in the recall test – the opposite of the predicted result, and an absence of recognition failure.

Why did this experiment fail to replicate the finding of recognition failure?  One possible reason is that the study session did not create an adequately strong association between the cue and target.  Bryant (1991) reported evidence that the phenomenon of recognition failure depends on forming a strong association between cue and target.  Another possible reason for the failure to find recognition failure is that the distractors in the recognition test were not presented adjacent to their associated target words.  Adjacent related distractors would have been more likely to combine with the target words and therefore decrease the similarity between the interpretation of the target in the recognition list and the memory trace of the target formed during the study phase.  A third possibility is that the participants were perverse.  Just wondered if you actually read this or not.
